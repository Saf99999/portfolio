Built a 400-person school's entire AI governance framework from zero — policy, guidance, training — over 20 months, with no off-the-shelf model to adapt.

Initiative Lead300 students · 100+ staff3 stakeholder groups with competing concerns

Situation

By late 2024, generative AI was already woven into daily life at the school. Students were leaning on it for assignments, teachers were unsure what counted as acceptable, and leadership could see the risk building. There was no policy, no shared standard for evaluating tools, and no common language for what responsible use even meant. Adoption was happening regardless — the only real choice was whether the school would shape it or be shaped by it.

Complication

The hard part wasn't the technology. Three groups held genuinely competing concerns: faculty worried about academic integrity, parents worried about safety, students worried about access and fairness. Any policy that satisfied one group risked alienating another, and no off-the-shelf framework fit a school of this size and context. Moving too slowly meant the informal free-for-all continued; moving too fast risked a policy nobody trusted or followed.

Approach

  • Researched the landscape before committing to a direction — benchmarked established AI frameworks and peer international schools' published guidance, attended AI conferences, reviewed academic literature. Synthesized the strongest elements into guiding principles adapted from the EDSAFE AI SAFE Framework and UNESCO guidance.
  • Engaged faculty, students, and parents directly through focus groups, surfacing competing concerns early and using them to shape the policy rather than defend against them later.
  • Made progress without a budget: developed a funding proposal for the school's preferred platform (FLINT); when it wasn't approved, ran a structured pilot of FLINT alongside other candidate tools and standardized on FLINT's free tier — proving value first and removing cost as a barrier.
  • Secured Canva for Education for the school, extending the initiative's impact into the marketing department's operations as well.
  • Designed a two-part governance architecture: a concise AI Policy (principles and roles) paired with a practical Guidance Document teachers could use day to day — tiered acceptable-use rubrics for elementary and secondary with a visual logo system, formal/informal citation standards, a vetted tool list, and an EdTech vetting rubric with a Technology Committee approval path.
  • Built a fair, evidence-based integrity process: a step-by-step misuse protocol leading with student dialogue, treating AI-detector scores as unreliable and never sole evidence, escalating through existing academic-integrity channels only when warranted.
  • Sequenced the rollout deliberately — policy developed through June 2025, implemented August 2025.
  • Led capability building through hands-on teacher training on FLINT and approved tools, culminating in a school-hosted technology conference (November 2025).

Result

  • A governing AI Policy (v1.0) and companion Guidance Document — the school's first shared AI standard.
  • Tiered acceptable-use rubrics with a visual logo system, plus citation standards giving every classroom one consistent language for AI.
  • A vetted, regularly reviewed tool list and committee process for evaluating new ones.
  • A teacher-facing misuse-response protocol protecting students from false accusations while upholding integrity.
  • Over 100 staff and 300 students operating under shared expectations, reinforced by a standing PD model.
  • A built-in quarterly review cycle, sustained under continued leadership through June 2026.

Led a whole-school SIS migration (Gibbon → PowerSchool) across 700 people and nine stakeholder groups, hitting a fixed go-live date.

Implementation Lead / SIS Coordinator600 students · 100+ staffGo-live: January 19, 2026 — hit

Situation

CISGZ was running on Gibbon, an SIS that had reached the limits of what it could support as the school grew. Leadership committed to replacing it with PowerSchool, framed around a clear change vision: "PowerSchool will provide a reliable foundation for managing student information — streamlining processes, reducing inefficiencies, and improving accuracy. With clearer systems and stronger support, staff, students, and families can focus on what matters most: student learning and success."

Complication

This wasn't a single-team rollout. Deliverables touched nine distinct stakeholder groups: leadership needed governance and funding oversight, teachers needed gradebook and attendance tools many had never used, office and admissions staff needed records and intake workflows rebuilt from scratch, counselling staff needed graduation tracking and provincial (PASI) reporting to keep working, and parents and students needed portal access that didn't exist before. All of it had to be sequenced against a fixed go-live date tied to the start of term, while migrating years of legacy data out of Gibbon — contact data migration was the slowest-moving piece.

Approach

  • Defined a phased scope statement sequencing base setup and data migration ahead of training or go-live, instead of configuring and training under pressure in parallel.
  • Sequenced go-live around what staff needed first: attendance tracking and PowerTeacher Pro grade input, both live school-wide by January 19, 2026.
  • Built role-specific training rather than one-size-fits-all: administrative/data/communication training for leadership and office staff in December 2025, separate PowerTeacher Pro training for teaching staff in January 2026.
  • Mapped every deliverable to a named, accountable stakeholder — from the executive sponsor down to IT and parents — so governance didn't depend on informal follow-up.

Result

CISGZ moved off Gibbon onto a live, school-wide PowerSchool platform: attendance tracking and PowerTeacher Pro grade input running school-wide from go-live, leadership and staff trained on their respective functions, and a governance structure spanning nine stakeholder groups. PASI integration with Alberta Education, the first PowerSchool-generated report card, and PowerScheduler configuration for 2026–27 are the focus for next year.

Designed and delivered a hands-on AI prompting workshop for UN staff in Eritrea, built around an original framework (GCSE+I) — leadership noted visible improvement afterward.

AI Workshop Facilitator8–10 staff, mixed experienceMaterials distributed to other UN offices

Situation

Staff were already using generative AI for policy briefs, communications, and operational work, but with no shared standard for how to prompt effectively or judge output. Early use was inconsistent — some staff got strong results, most got generic, unreliable drafts because the prompts behind them gave the model nothing to work with.

Complication

This was a skills gap, not a technology problem, and a one-off demo wouldn't close it. Staff needed something concrete enough to apply immediately to their own work (policy briefs, M&E reports, comms), but simple enough to remember without a reference doc in hand. The session also had to land for a mixed-experience audience in a single sitting.

Approach

  • Designed and delivered "From Prompts to Personalization: Using AI Effectively," built around an original five-move framework — GCSE+I (Goal, Context, Sources, Expectations, +Iterate) — rather than a generic tool overview.
  • Opened with a live demo contrasting a vague prompt against the same request rebuilt with GCSE+I, making the quality difference immediate and visible.
  • Ran a hands-on table exercise: participants rewrote their own real prompts using the framework and iterated live.
  • Extended the framework beyond single prompts into Custom GPTs and Copilot Agents, with worked examples (a Policy GPT for briefs, an M&E GPT for logframe tables, an Ops GPT for meeting-notes-to-action-logs).
  • Closed with explicit guardrails: data handling, source grounding, accountability for verifying AI output, and a maintenance norm (review shared agents monthly or per milestone).
  • Backed the session with leave-behind resources: a one-page GCSE+I reference card, a prompting cheat sheet, and a written guide to building Custom GPTs/Copilot Agents.

Result

Feedback was positive, and the Resident Coordinator specifically noted visible improvements in staff work following the session — direct evidence the framework changed how people worked, not just how they felt about the training. Materials were distributed to other UN offices in Eritrea afterward.

Inherited a 12-year-old, under-invested SIS with no budget appetite — secured $25K in funding and rebuilt it into a reliable operational backbone over 4 years.

PowerSchool AdministratorSchool-wide: attendance, grading, reporting, scheduling200+ hrs implementation training

Situation

When taking on the PowerSchool Administrator role in 2020, the system had been running for twelve years with no meaningful reinvestment. Configuration was outdated, workflows were inconsistent, and there was no documentation or formal training behind how the school actually used it. The school depended on the system daily for attendance, grading, and reporting, but nobody had ever been resourced to make it reliable.

Complication

There was no budget allocated for an upgrade, and no appetite to spend on a system that wasn't visibly broken — even though the data accuracy and workflow problems underneath it were compounding. Administrators and counsellors still needed to trust the system for graduation planning and student support.

Approach

  • Built the case for investment and secured $25,000 USD in funding to properly upgrade the platform rather than patch around its limitations.
  • Completed 200+ hours of system implementation training, building the technical depth to fully reconfigure the SIS.
  • Led the upgrade and reconfiguration, redesigning workflows for data accuracy, consistency, and usability.
  • Built documentation that hadn't existed before, removing reliance on institutional memory.
  • Created standard operating procedures for every group using the system.
  • Delivered staff training to drive adoption of corrected workflows.
  • Configured the system for multi-year scheduling, extending it from single-year operation to long-range academic planning.
  • Partnered directly with administrators and counsellors so SIS data could be trusted for graduation tracking and reporting decisions.

Result

Over four years: improved data accuracy and workflow consistency school-wide, a funded and properly trained implementation in place of ad hoc fixes, documentation and training that outlasted any single person's tenure, and a system administrators and counsellors could actually depend on.

Signature framework

GCSE+I · Goal, Context, Sources, Expectations, +Iterate

Most AI prompts are vague, and vague prompts produce generic output. GCSE+I is a five-move structure built to fix that systematically — for teams, not just individual prompting.

First applied training UN staff (Resident Coordinator's Office, Eritrea), then carried into AI guidance and training design for a 400-person school community. Same logic in both settings: AI output is only as good as the structure behind the request — and that structure can be taught.

G

Goal

State the exact outcome you want.

"Draft a 150-word brief for a non-technical audience."
C

Context

Add background — who, where, why.

"For UN Eritrea staff; focus on 2025 priorities."
S

Sources

Point to the specific facts the model should use.

"Use attached PDF pp. 3–5 and this URL."
E

Expectations

Define format, tone, must-haves.

"Bullets; neutral tone; 3 risks & 3 mitigations; max 180 words."
+I

Iterate

Tighten the prompt and output after each draft.

"Shorten the intro; add one data point; keep the risks section."
Vague

"Write about climate adaptation funding."

GCSE+I

"You are a UN policy analyst. Goal: draft a 150-word brief for senior leadership in plain language. Context: East Africa, 2025 priorities. Sources: use paragraphs 2–4 of the attached memo only. Expectations: bullets, 3 key risks and 3 mitigations, one short call-to-action, neutral tone. Constraints: no external sources."

The difference isn't length — the second version removes every ambiguity the model would otherwise have to guess at. The human step: AI output is a draft, not a deliverable. GCSE+I ends with the user checking, refining, and owning the result — not handing off accountability to the model. Beyond individual prompting, the framework extends into Custom GPTs and Copilot Agents, so a team doesn't re-explain the same context every time.
